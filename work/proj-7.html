SITUATION

Navigating with maps (physical or digital) can be intimidating for some users. However, there is a possibility to integrate a game element to make users more comfortable. The Physical Prototyping & Computing module motivated me to explore whether this can be done using physical prototypes.

TASK

I set a task for myself to build a physical map reader with a geography game in order to encourage users to learn some geography and to feel comfortable utilizing paper maps.


ACTION
1. Ideation

ScopIt evolved from multiple rounds of ideation. My initial concepts revolved around wayfinding and map navigation. One idea consisted of smart shoes in-soles, “Shoe N’ Tell”, that assists navigation by vibrating on the left or right foot. I have also brainstormed other concepts, such as a device that ‘reads’ the Tube map and notifies its user of delays happening in the underground.

1a. Hardware Exploration

Upon learning how the RFID reader can detect RFID tags in different mediums, I decided to stick with my second concept: the map reader. I envisioned attaching NFC stickers at the back of a paper map and allowing my prototype to detect these stickers and transmit certain information - as if it was 'reading' the map.


RFID tags found in different mediums

It was difficult for the RFID reader to detect NFC stickers that are placed close to each other. I then refined my idea into a geography game, which presents questions to users (one at a time) to locate famous landmarks. The following storyboard indicates how a child might use this device in a school or museum.

2. Prototyping
2a. Digital Fabrication

Two prototyping process happened in parallel to each other. The first is digital fabrication, where I tried to explore to form factor of my physical prototype. One idea is to build a magnifying glass, which relates to the gamification idea of searching where the landmarks are. Another concept was a monocle that users can move around the map. For quick and easy prototyping, I started out using cardboards and foams to explore the look and feel of these forms.

2c. Combining Form and Electronics

This combination brought some design implications. I quickly realized that the bulkiness of the electronics would affect my form factors. For example, I feared that if I were to use the magnifying glass concept, the handle might break due to the hardware’s weight. This bulkiness also constrained users to hold the prototype with two hands – unlike what I have imagined with the light weight monocle.



Informed by my combined cardboard mockup, I created an acrylic container to hold my electronics together. I have considered 3D printing this container. But after speculating on how few cutouts I actually need, I chose to laser cut my design. The short time duration for laser cutting motivated my approach since I plan on iterating my form’s design.

Iterated prototype design

3. Evaluation & Iteration

In my third prototype, I also added a slant for the LCD display so that it allows easier viewing for the reader. I enclosed the electronic parts and wirings inside a cloth so no pieces would fall out of the prototype. The RFID reader was sticked at the bottom, outside of the prototype, since it detects the NFC tags better with no materials in-between (such as the acrylic plastic).

RESULTS
Future Work

A proper prototype evaluation and user evaluation are required to see how ScopIt is used in-the-wild. Current form factor could easily change, depending on how physical maps are laid out and how user interacts with them. Some maps are displayed vertically in museums, on the streets, or classroom walls. ScopIt’s current form factor is appropriate for maps placed across horizontal surfaces. Revisiting the magnifying glass concept would be worthwhile. This concept is suitable for maps displayed on vertical surfaces.
